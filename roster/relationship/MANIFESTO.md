# The Relationship Butler

## A Friend Who Never Forgets

In a world of endless distractions and infinite inboxes, the people who matter most often get the leftover attention we have time for. We mean well—we genuinely want to remember Sarah's new job, call Mom on her birthday, follow up with the friend we had coffee with last Tuesday—but life moves fast, and good intentions fade.

The Relationship Butler is your external memory for the people you care about. It's the friend who remembers everything so you don't have to forget anything that matters.

## What It Does

The Relationship Butler is a personal CRM built around three simple ideas:

**Thoughtfulness:** Never miss an important date. Set it once, and the butler reminds you when birthdays and anniversaries approach. Track gift ideas as they come to you—one person, one wish list—and move them from "idea" to "purchased" to "given" without the burden of keeping it all in your head.

**Richness:** Notes aren't just facts. The butler captures emotion—was the conversation joyful, sad, inspiring? It tracks who you talk to and when, builds a history of your interactions, and remembers the loans you've made, the meaningful conversations you've had, and the small details that make relationships real. A complete picture of the people in your life.

**Connection:** By remembering the details, the butler helps you be more present in the moment. You're not scrambling to recall context or worried you'll forget something important. You show up more thoughtfully. You listen more carefully. You build deeper, more intentional relationships.

## Why It Matters

Life moves in cycles. Someone mentions a new hobby, and you forget to ask about it next time. You think of a friend and realize it's been three months. You meant to thank someone properly but never got around to it. A small child becomes a person you haven't heard from in a year.

These aren't failures of character—they're failures of memory systems. The Relationship Butler fixes that. It gives you superpowers of recall so you can focus on what actually matters: being a good friend, a thoughtful family member, a reliable person in other people's lives.

## What You Get

- **Contact management:** A living database of the people in your life—their interests, preferences, and history with you.
- **Important dates:** Birthdays, anniversaries, milestones—never surprised, always prepared.
- **Gift tracking:** From "I should get them something" to "that went well," organized and never forgotten.
- **Interaction history:** A feed of who you've talked to and when, so context is always at hand.
- **Relationship notes:** Capture everything that matters—emotion, context, observations—in a way that feels natural.
- **Financial clarity:** Track loans, shared expenses, or money you've lent so no awkwardness lingers.
- **Smart reminders:** One-time follow-ups or recurring check-ins so you stay connected without thinking about it.
- **Flexible organization:** Group contacts by role (family, close friends, colleagues), label them by context, and search by relationship.

## The Philosophy

The Relationship Butler is built on the belief that good relationships require attention, but attention shouldn't be a burden. It shouldn't require spreadsheets, elaborate note-taking systems, or constant cognitive overhead. The butler handles the memory work. You handle the connection work.

It's about being the friend who shows up. The person people feel genuinely cared for by. The one who remembers. The one who follows through. The one who notices when someone has been quiet for a while.

And it all starts with never forgetting what matters.

### The Dunbar Model

Human social capacity is not flat. Research by Robin Dunbar shows that relationships exist in concentric layers of intensity, each roughly three times the size of the one inside it:

| Layer | Size | Who they are |
|-------|------|--------------|
| **Support clique** | ~5 | Intimate friends. The people you'd call at 3am. |
| **Sympathy group** | ~15 | Close friends. You'd be genuinely distressed by their loss. |
| **Good friends** | ~50 | People you'd invite to a group dinner without hesitation. |
| **Meaningful contacts** | ~150 | Your active social network. People you maintain real relationships with. |
| **Acquaintances** | ~500 | People you know and could have a conversation with. |
| **Recognizable** | ~1500 | People you can put a name to a face. |

The Relationship Butler uses this structure as its foundational model for how it prioritizes your attention. Not all contacts are equal — the butler knows this, and it acts accordingly.

**Attention flows inward.** Inner-circle relationships get more frequent check-ins, more context in suggestions, and higher priority in weekly reviews. A fading friendship at the 5-layer matters more than a stale acquaintance at the 500-layer, and the butler reflects that in every recommendation it makes.

**Relationships decay without interaction.** Every person has an implicit health score that erodes over time. When you interact with someone — log a call, send a message, meet for coffee — their score refreshes. When you don't, it fades. The butler doesn't nag about this decay; it simply lets it influence who surfaces in your weekly suggestions and in what order. Reach-out reminders are the natural mechanism that arrests the drift.

**Tier placement is implicit, not administered.** The butler infers which layer someone belongs to based on interaction frequency and recency — not by asking you to sort 150 people into buckets. If you talk to someone every week, they're in your inner circle. If you haven't spoken in a year, they've drifted outward. Manual overrides exist for the cases where the math doesn't match reality — a sibling you rarely call but who is unquestionably in your support clique.

**Layer sizes are fixed constraints, not preferences.** The 5-15-50-150-500-1500 numbers come from cognitive science, not user configuration. If the system shows 20 people at the support-clique level, that's honest feedback about social overextension, not a setting to adjust.
